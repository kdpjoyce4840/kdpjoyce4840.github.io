Reflections

These reflections highlight what I’m learning as I develop skills in instructional design and educational technology—what worked, what I’d change, and how I’m applying research-informed practices to create accessible, engaging learning experiences.

Designing for Accessibility First

I’ve adopted an accessibility-first mindset. For each page and activity, I walk through a quick checklist that includes descriptive titles and logical heading order, sufficient color contrast, meaningful alt text, keyboard navigability with visible focus states, and clear link styles and microcopy.

This approach improves clarity and reduces cognitive load for all learners—not just those who rely on assistive technology.

Structuring Content for Sense-Making

I’m experimenting with chunking, advance organizers, and progressive disclosure to support comprehension. A consistent layout with short intros, clear objectives, and small, “do-able” tasks helps learners know exactly where they are and what’s next.

I’ve also tightened typography and spacing to make scanning easier, using gentle dividers and consistent margins to guide the eye.

Iterating with Feedback

Peer and instructor feedback shapes my process. I prototype quickly, test with a few users, and iterate: sketch for structure, style for look and feel, then refine language and verify accessibility. This keeps me focused on learner needs while improving quality each cycle.

What’s Next

I plan to extend this site with interactive learning components and richer project write-ups, including rationale, standards alignment, and evidence of impact. If you’d like to collaborate or discuss ideas, feel free to reach out.
