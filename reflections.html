Reflections

Learning, practice, and growth across design and technology.

My path into instructional design has moved from elementary classrooms and counseling rooms to a software support desk. These reflections capture what I’m learning as I blend those experiences into clearer, more compassionate learning experiences.

Designing for Accessibility First

I’ve adopted an accessibility-first mindset. Whether I’m writing an SOP for our My Vision Express users or building a module for grad school, I start by asking how someone with limited time, energy, or access will experience it.

My checklist includes descriptive page titles, a logical heading order (H1 → H2 → H3), strong color contrast, meaningful alt text, keyboard navigability with visible focus states, and clear, consistent link styles.

I’ve noticed a real impact: fewer “where do I click?” questions from users, smoother scanning for busy staff, and content that holds up better on small screens and assistive tech. Accessibility work has started to feel less like a chore and more like a professional habit.

Structuring Content for Sense-Making

Years of teaching elementary students and counseling adolescents taught me that people rarely struggle because they’re “not trying.” More often, the instructions or structure simply ask too much at once.

I’ve leaned into chunking and progressive disclosure—short intros, explicit objectives, and “do-able” steps that reduce cognitive load. Using consistent spacing, gentle dividers, and predictable patterns makes the interface feel calmer. Learners don’t spend energy figuring out the layout; they spend it on the task.

When I rewrite content for clarity, I hear fewer apologies like “Sorry, I’m just bad with computers.” That’s when I know the structure is finally doing some of the emotional labor instead of the learner.

Iterating with Feedback

Feedback loops are now part of my normal workflow. In support, this might mean testing a draft SOP with another tech. In my course work, it looks like sharing wireframes or draft modules with classmates before I polish the visuals.

My rhythm looks like this: sketch for structure → apply palette + spacing → refine language and interactions → verify accessibility. Each pass gets me closer to something clear and usable instead of just “pretty.”

Coming from teaching, I used to feel pressure to get things “right” the first time. Now I’m more comfortable treating every design as a living document that gets better with each real interaction.

Tools & Workflow

Beyond HTML/CSS and GitHub Pages, I’m building a small toolkit for reliable delivery: version control with Git, quick wireframes in Figma (or pencil), contrast checks, keyboard testing, and lightweight style guides.

The goal is speed with quality—get something in front of users fast, then keep polishing. That mindset has helped me balance the realities of support tickets, deadlines, and grad school projects without losing sight of the learner.

What’s Next

Looking ahead, I plan to extend this site with interactive learning components, richer project write-ups, and clearer links between each artifact and specific learning outcomes or competencies.

I’m especially interested in designing resources for adolescents affected by substance use and for grandparents raising grandchildren—drawing on my counseling background with my current focus on learning technologies.
